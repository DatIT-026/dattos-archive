Lumia 520: My First Smartphone

Microsoft officially pulled the plug on Windows Phone on January 14, 2020, right as the legendary Windows 7 was being phased out to make way for a new generation. Back then, Lumia devices were custom-built by Nokia and Microsoft specifically to run on this platform. For me, the Lumia 520 wasn’t just a popular budget phone, it was my very first smartphone, the one that introduced me to a whole new world of technology. Fast forward to 2024, with AI dominating every conversation, I suddenly felt a wave of nostalgia. Since my old 520 had long since given up the ghost, I decided to pick up a Lumia 640 XL to see how this “relic” holds up in today’s digital world.

The legendary Lumia 520, the gateway that led me into the world of technology

Overview

My first smartphone, the Nokia Lumia 520, always holds a very special place in my tech journey. I still clearly remember how excited I was when unboxing it in the afternoon sun, looking at its bright plastic cover that could be easily removed and swapped. Even though it was a budget phone, it felt solid in my hand, compact, and very well-made.

What impressed me the most was its pure simplicity. The Live Tiles interface on Windows Phone 8.1 looked modern and easy to use. It was a unique style that only the Lumia line had. The phone’s performance was very smooth, which was more than enough for all my basic needs at that time.

Even though the 5 MP camera looks “old” compared to today’s standards, back then it felt like high-tech. It was the tool that helped me capture everyday moments in the easiest way possible.

Lumia 640 XL: A Successor from the Past

As Windows Phone faded into the past, I happened to read about the Windows 11 ARM project for old devices, which immediately made me think of the Lumia series. Curious about the fate of a platform that failed despite its potential, I bought a Lumia 640 XL to see how it survives today.

In 2010, Microsoft employees held a funeral for the iPhone and BlackBerry after launching Windows Phone 7.

Holding the Lumia 640 XL next to today’s bezel-less smartphones, the first thing I noticed was how surprisingly solid it feels. Although the 5.7-inch screen resolution isn’t very high, the IPS panel and ClearBlack technology keep the colors sharp. The deep blacks, in particular, make the Live Tiles really stand out.

Photography with ZEISS Lens

What I expected most was the 13 MP camera with a ZEISS lens. In good lighting, the photos have a level of detail and dynamic range (DR) that quite surprised me. It carries a very distinct color signature, not overly processed by AI like today’s phone models.

4. “Surviving” with a Dead Operating System

The question is: What can a machine running Windows 10 Mobile do in 2024?

App Status in 2024
Browser Monument Browser still handles light news sites well.
Messaging Unigram (Telegram client) is the only lifeline for communication.
Entertainment Offline music via SD card remains the ultimate choice.

The reality is that most popular apps like Facebook or YouTube have stopped working. However, thanks to the community of developers who are still passionate, we can use web wrappers or third-party apps to maintain the most basic features.

5. Crazy Project: Windows 11 ARM on Lumia

This is the most dramatic part. I couldn’t just let this machine sit quietly with its ancient OS. I experimented with installing the WOA (Windows on ARM) project on this 640 XL.

Source: Internet

The feeling of seeing the Windows 11 Start button appear on a phone from 2015 is truly hard to describe. Although the machine runs extremely hot and lags due to hardware limits, it proves one thing: The limit of technology only lies in our imagination.

Conclusion

The journey from Lumia 520 to 640 XL isn’t just about swapping for a better phone, but it is how I keep a connection to memories from when I was just starting out in the Software Engineering industry.

While it cannot be used as a primary device, whenever I need a quiet space to focus or simply want to feel nostalgic about the “stone age” of smartphones, I pick up this Lumia. Windows Phone may be commercially dead, but its soul lives on in the community of true tech lovers.
